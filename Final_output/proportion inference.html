In the foundations chapter we developed the logic of statistical inference — null and alternative hypotheses, the two types of error, the significance level, the p-value and critical region approaches to making a decision, and the idea of a confidence interval — without performing any computations. That reasoning does not depend on which parameter we study. In this chapter we put it to work for a population proportion \(p\), filling in the parameter-specific machinery the foundations chapter deferred: the sampling distribution of the count \(X\), the calculations that produce a p-value and a critical region, and the software steps to carry them out in Rguroo. We treat both the one-population and two-population proportion settings.

Because the conceptual framework is already in place, this chapter focuses on computation. We will recall each idea briefly as we use it, with a pointer back to the foundations chapter for the full discussion, and spend our effort on how the ideas are actually carried out for a proportion. The same pattern will repeat in the next chapter for a population mean: new computations, the same framework.

We begin with hypothesis testing for one population proportion.

8.1 Hypothesis Testing: Concepts and Setup

In many real situations, we encounter numerical claims about population proportions: the proportion of teenagers who use a particular app, the proportion of adults who meet health guidelines, or the proportion of students who rely on social media for news. But how can we determine whether such claims are reasonable? When does sample evidence support a claim, and when should we question it?

To see how we might answer such questions, we will work through examples and develop the reasoning needed to evaluate claims and reach conclusions. We begin by establishing the framework and notation we’ll use throughout this chapter.

8.1.1 Setting the Stage: A Running Example

Suppose we want to know what proportion of students at our university prefer online classes. The population is all students at our university. The response variable is class mode preference, a categorical variable (also called a factor) that classifies each student into one of two categories: prefers online classes or does not prefer online classes. We define “prefers online classes” as a success and the other category as a failure.

The parameter of interest is the population proportion, denoted by \(p\)—the fraction of all students at our university who prefer online classes. Since we cannot survey every student, we collect data from a random sample and use it to learn about \(p\).

This example illustrates the key elements of proportion inference:

  • A population with a characteristic we want to measure
  • A categorical response variable with two outcomes (success/failure)
  • A parameter \(p\) representing the true population proportion
  • A sample from which we compute a statistic to estimate \(p\)

Let’s develop this framework more formally.

8.1.2 From Samples to Statistics

When we collect a sample of size \(n\), we count how many individuals have a success outcome. Before the sample is collected, this count is unknown, so we treat it as a random variable and denote it by \(X\). After the data are collected, we observe the actual count, which we denote by \(x_{\text{obs}}\).

In our example, suppose we randomly select \(n = 100\) students. Before we collect the sample, \(X\) represents the number of students (out of 100) who will say they prefer online classes—this could be anywhere from 0 to 100. After we survey the students, suppose 62 say they prefer online classes. Then the observed count is \(x_{\text{obs}} = 62\).

\(X\) is a random variable representing the number of successes across all possible samples; \(x_{\text{obs}}\) is the specific count from our actual sample. We use \(x_{\text{obs}}\) to make decisions about \(p\).

The count \(X\) can also be converted to a proportion. The sample proportion \(\hat{p} = X/n\) (read “p-hat”) gives the fraction of individuals in the sample with the characteristic of interest and serves as a natural estimate of \(p\). Like \(X\), the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) is a random variable—it varies from sample to sample. Once we have our data, the observed sample proportion \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} = x_{\text{obs}}/n\) is the specific value from our sample. In our example, \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} = 62/100 = 0.62\).

8.1.3 The Distribution of \(X\)

To use \(X\) for inference, we need to know its probability distribution. Since we are sampling from a population and counting successes, the binomial distribution provides the appropriate model. Recall from Section 5.2 that a random variable follows a binomial distribution when:

  • The number of trials \(n\) is fixed
  • Each trial results in one of two outcomes: success or failure
  • The probability of success \(p\) is the same for every trial
  • The trials are independent of one another

In our sampling context, each individual in the sample either has the characteristic of interest (success) or does not (failure). Since the sample is selected randomly, the trials are independent. The probability of success \(p\) is constant—it equals the population proportion. (Strictly speaking, sampling without replacement causes \(p\) to change slightly with each draw, but when the sample is small relative to the population, this change is negligible and we treat \(p\) as constant.)

Therefore, \[ X \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p). \]

This distribution will be the foundation for computing probabilities and making decisions about \(p\).

  4. The parameter of interest \(p\)

  6. The statistic \(X\) and its observed value \(x_{\text{obs}}\)

To answer whether our sample results would be unlikely to occur by chance if the claims were false, we need a formal framework. We also need to acknowledge that because we make decisions using only a sample, there is always some uncertainty and a chance of being wrong. We might conclude that the evidence supports a claim when in fact it does not, or we might fail to support a claim that is actually true.

8.1.4 Null and Alternative Hypotheses and Errors for a Proportion

The foundations chapter (Section 7.3) developed the null and alternative hypotheses, the three forms they take, and the two types of error in full. Here we recall just enough to fix notation for a proportion and then apply it to our running examples.

For a population proportion, the alternative hypothesis \(H_a\) is the claim we seek evidence for, and the null hypothesis \(H_0\) is its complement, the value or range we assume true unless the data force us to abandon it. Both are statements about the population proportion \(p\), not about the sample, so they are written using \(p\) and not \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\). Writing \(p_0\) for the boundary value that separates the null from the alternative, the three forms are:

\[ \text{Case 1: } \begin{cases} H_0: p \leq p_0 \\ H_a: p > p_0 \end{cases} \qquad \text{Case 2: } \begin{cases} H_0: p \geq p_0 \\ H_a: p < p_0 \end{cases} \qquad \text{Case 3: } \begin{cases} H_0: p = p_0 \\ H_a: p \neq p_0 \end{cases} \]

Cases 1 and 2 are one-sided and Case 3 is two-sided, exactly as in Section 7.3.1.

Recall too the two ways a test can be wrong: a Type I error rejects \(H_0\) when \(H_0\) is in fact true, and a Type II error fails to reject \(H_0\) when \(H_a\) is in fact true (see Section 7.4). The following examples write the hypotheses and interpret both errors for our three proportion scenarios.

Example 8.4 (Daily YouTube Use Among Teens (continued)) Recall Example 8.1 where a school district was interested to test the claim that more than 70% of students in its district use YouTube daily.

  1. State the null and alternative hypotheses in words.
  2. Write the null and alternative hypotheses using mathematical notation.
  3. Describe what happens if a Type I error occurs in the context of the problem.
  4. Describe what happens if a Type II error occurs in the context of the problem.
🔎 Solution

  1. The null hypothesis states that 70% or fewer students in the district use YouTube daily. The alternative hypothesis states that more than 70% of students in the district use YouTube daily.

  2. \[ \begin{cases} H_0: p \leq 0.70 \\ H_a: p > 0.70 \end{cases} \]

8.2 Making Decisions in Hypothesis Testing

In some situations, it is easy to see that the sample evidence does not lead us to reject \(H_0\). For example, in the YouTube case, the hypotheses are \(H_0: p \leq 0.70\) versus \(H_a: p > 0.70\). If a sample of \(n = 20\) students had resulted in only \(x_{\text{obs}} = 12\) students reporting daily use, then \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} = \frac{12}{20} = 0.60\). With only 12 out of 20 students, the sample proportion \(0.60\) is well below \(0.70\) and points in the direction of \(H_0\), not \(H_a\). There is nothing here that could lead us to reject \(H_0\).

Similarly, in the physical inactivity example with \(H_0: p \geq 0.35\) versus \(H_a: p < 0.35\), if a sample of \(n = 40\) adults had shown \(x_{\text{obs}} = 20\) inactive individuals, then \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} = \frac{20}{40} = 0.50\). A sample proportion of \(0.50\) sits comfortably inside the range allowed by \(H_0\) and gives no reason to reject it.

In the social media example, with \(H_0: p = 0.36\) versus \(H_a: p \neq 0.36\), it is less obvious which values of \(x_{\text{obs}}\) would lead us to reject \(H_0\). Counts that produce sample proportions very close to \(0.36\) would not give us grounds to reject \(H_0\), but it is not yet clear how far from \(0.36\) the sample proportion must fall before we would reject.

Now consider the actual sample results. In each case, the count \(x_{\text{obs}}\) and the corresponding sample proportion are consistent with \(H_a\). For the YouTube example (\(H_a: p > 0.70\)), \(x_{\text{obs}} = 17\) out of \(n = 20\) gives \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} = 0.85\), which is greater than \(0.70\). For the physical inactivity example (\(H_a: p < 0.35\)), \(x_{\text{obs}} = 10\) out of \(n = 50\) gives \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} = 0.20\), which is less than \(0.35\). For the social media example (\(H_a: p \neq 0.36\)), \(x_{\text{obs}} = 9\) out of \(n = 15\) gives \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} = 0.60\), which differs by a good amount from \(0.36\). Each result is consistent with \(H_a\). So are we done? Can we reject \(H_0\) in each case?

Not necessarily!

Consistency With \(H_a\) Is Not the Same as Strong Evidence in Favor of \(H_a\)

A sample result that is consistent with \(H_a\) does not by itself constitute strong evidence in favor of \(H_a\). Because of sampling variability, such a result can occur by chance even when \(H_0\) is true. The key question is whether the sample result would be unlikely to occur by chance if \(H_0\) were true — only then do we have strong evidence in favor of \(H_a\).

We now turn to actually carrying out a test for a proportion. Recall from Section 7.4.2 that, because reducing one type of error tends to increase the other, we control the probability of a Type I error by holding it to a small, prespecified significance level \(\alpha\), and that there are two equivalent ways to reach a decision: the p-value approach and the critical region approach, which always agree. This section shows how each is computed for a proportion, using the binomial distribution of the count \(X\). We present the p-value approach first, since it is the default output of most statistical software and is the most widely reported in practice.

8.2.1 The p-Value Approach

The p-value approach measures how unusual the observed count \(x_{\text{obs}}\) would be if the null hypothesis were true, and rejects \(H_0\) when the data are sufficiently unusual under \(H_0\). To make this concrete for a proportion, we evaluate probabilities at the boundary value \(p_0\) (see Section 7.3.2), using the binomial distribution of the count \(X\).

Recall the \(H_a\) support from Section 7.5: the values of \(X\) that are at least as supportive of \(H_a\) as the observed value \(x_{\text{obs}}\). Using this definition, the p-value for a proportion is

\[ \text{p-value} = P(X \text{ is a value in the } H_a \text{ support. assuming} p = p_0). \]

A small p-value is evidence against \(H_0\).

What counts as “the \(H_a\) support” depends on the form of the alternative hypothesis.

Case 1: \(H_a: p > p_0\) (e.g., \(H_a: p > 0.70\))

Larger values of \(X\) are more supportive of \(H_a\). The \(H_a\) support consists of \(x_{\text{obs}}\) and all values larger than \(x_{\text{obs}}\), so the p-value is \[ \text{p-value} = P(X \geq x_{\text{obs}} \mid p = p_0). \]

To understand Case 1, note that a larger value of \(X\) means more successes in the sample, which corresponds to a higher sample proportion \(\hat{p}\). A higher sample proportion is less consistent with \(H_0\) and more supportive of the claim that the true population proportion exceeds \(p_0\). For example, in the YouTube example where \(x_{\text{obs}} = 17\) out of \(n = 20\), the \(H_a\) support consists of the values \(\{17, 18, 19, 20\}\) — the observed count and every larger possibility.

Case 2: \(H_a: p < p_0\) (e.g., \(H_a: p < 0.35\))

Smaller values of \(X\) are more supportive of \(H_a\). The \(H_a\) support consists of \(x_{\text{obs}}\) and all values smaller than \(x_{\text{obs}}\), so the p-value is \[ \text{p-value} = P(X \leq x_{\text{obs}} \mid p = p_0). \]

Case 2 is the mirror image of Case 1: a smaller value of \(X\) means fewer successes in the sample, which corresponds to a lower sample proportion \(\hat{p}\). A lower sample proportion is less consistent with \(H_0\) and more supportive of the claim that the true population proportion is less than \(p_0\). For example, in the Physical Inactivity example where \(x_{\text{obs}} = 10\) out of \(n = 50\), the \(H_a\) support is \(\{0, 1, 2, \ldots, 9, 10\}\) — the observed count and every smaller possibility.

Case 3: \(H_a: p \neq p_0\) (e.g., \(H_a: p \neq 0.36\))

A value \(x\) is in the \(H_a\) support if it is at least as unlikely as the observed value \(x_{\text{obs}}\) under \(H_0\), that is, \[ P(X = x \mid p = p_0) \leq P(X = x_{\text{obs}} \mid p = p_0). \]

Such values can occur in either tail of the distribution.

The two-sided case is easiest to see in a picture. For the Social Media News example with \(x_{\text{obs}} = 9\) out of \(n = 15\) and \(p_0 = 0.36\), Figure 8.1 shows the binomial distribution of \(X\) under \(H_0\), where \(X \sim \text{Binomial}(15, 0.36)\).

The \(X\) values corresponding to the orange bars are the values of \(X\) whose probability under \(H_0\) is at most equal to \(P(X = 9)\). Therefore, the \(H_a\) support appears in both tails: large values \(9, 10, 11, \ldots\) on the right and small values \(0, 1\) on the left. The p-value is the total probability of the orange bars.

The decision rule using the p-value is straightforward:

Decision Rule Using the p-Value

\[ \text{If p-value} \leq \alpha, \text{ reject } H_0. \qquad \text{If p-value} > \alpha, \text{ fail to reject } H_0. \]

The reasoning behind this rule is simple: a small p-value means that the observed value \(x_{\text{obs}}\) and values at least as supportive of \(H_a\) would be unlikely to occur if \(p = p_0\) were true. If this probability is small enough, that is, at or below our chosen significance level \(\alpha\), we take it as sufficient evidence to reject \(H_0\). Conversely, a large p-value means that the observed result is not surprising under \(H_0\), and we do not have sufficient evidence to reject it.

The p-value approach has an advantage of reporting not just the decision but the strength of the evidence: a very small p-value indicates strong evidence against \(H_0\), while a p-value just below \(\alpha\) indicates weaker evidence.

8.2.2 The Critical Region Approach

We now turn to the critical region approach, the other standard way to reach a decision. Whereas the p-value approach summarizes the evidence with a probability computed from \(x_{\text{obs}}\), the critical region approach decides in advance which values of \(X\) are extreme enough to reject \(H_0\), and then simply checks whether \(x_{\text{obs}}\) is among them.

The decision is governed by a decision rule: a rule that specifies which values of \(X\) lead to rejecting \(H_0\). The rule aligns with the direction of \(H_a\), and takes one of three forms:

\[\text{Reject } H_0 \text{ if } \begin{cases} X \geq c & \text{when } H_a: p > p_0 \\ X \leq c & \text{when } H_a: p < p_0 \\ X \leq c_1 \text{ or } X \geq c_2 & \text{when } H_a: p \neq p_0 \end{cases}\] The values \(c\), \(c_1\), and \(c_2\) are called critical values, and the set of values of \(X\) that lead to rejecting \(H_0\) is the critical region (also called the rejection region).

The critical values are chosen so that the probability of a Type I error, computed at the boundary value \(p_0\) using \(X \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p_0)\), does not exceed the significance level \(\alpha\). Among all such choices, we use the largest critical region — that is, the one that rejects \(H_0\) for as many values of \(X\) as possible while still keeping the Type I error probability at or below \(\alpha\). This minimizes the probability of a Type II error.

In practice, we use Rguroo’s One-Population Proportion inference function to find this critical region.

Sometimes the Actual Significance Level Is Less Than \(\alpha\)

Because the binomial distribution is discrete, the count \(X\) takes only whole-number values, so the probability of the critical region jumps in discrete steps as we vary the cutoff. For most choices of \(n\), \(p_0\), and \(\alpha\) there is no critical region whose probability is exactly \(\alpha\). Rguroo therefore returns the largest critical region whose probability at the boundary is at or below \(\alpha\) — that is, the critical region whose actual significance level is as close to \(\alpha\) as possible without exceeding it; this is called Exact Significance Level in the output. For example, at the nominal level \(\alpha = 0.05\), Rguroo might return a critical region with actual significance level \(0.0476\) or \(0.0342\), both of which satisfy the criterion. This is standard practice for exact binomial tests; the actual significance level is reported alongside the critical region in the Rguroo output.

8.2.3 The Two Approaches Always Agree

As established in Section 7.6.1, the p-value approach and the critical region approach always give the same decision. For a proportion, the equivalence shows up concretely as three statements that are always either all true or all false:

  • The p-value is less than or equal to \(\alpha\).

  • \(x_{\text{obs}}\) falls in the critical region.

8.3 A Closer Look at Critical Regions and p-values **

Note

This section is optional. The material here is not required for performing hypothesis tests, but it is highly recommended for readers who want a firmer conceptual grasp of what is happening behind the procedures.

In the previous sections, you learned two equivalent approaches for testing a hypothesis about a population proportion: the critical region approach and the p-value approach. In both approaches, we computed probabilities for our decisions using the boundary value of the null hypothesis, and we relied on Rguroo’s Proportion Infrence function to produce the critical region or the p-value automatically.

This section is intended to deepen your understanding of why these procedures work the way they do. Specifically, we address two questions:

  1. Why do we use the boundary value of \(H_0\) when computing probabilities required for our decisions? The null hypothesis in many tests is a statement such as \(H_0: p \leq p_0\) or \(H_0: p \geq p_0\), which encompasses many possible values of \(p\). Yet we always use the single boundary value \(p_0\) in our calculations. There is a principled reason for this, and understanding it clarifies what a hypothesis test is really doing.

  2. How are critical regions and p-values actually obtained from a probability distribution? Rguroo’s Proportion Inference function returns these quantities directly, but doing so hides the underlying probability calculation. By using Rguroo’s probability calculator instead, we can see exactly which area under a sampling distribution corresponds to the critical region or the p-value.

8.3.1 Why Use the Boundary Value in \(H_0\)? **

In this section we explain why we use the boundary value of \(H_0\) when computing the probability of a Type I error. The front-half discussion stated this as a fact: for a composite \(H_0\) such as \(H_0: p \leq p_0\), the maximum Type I error probability over the values allowed by \(H_0\) occurs at the boundary \(p_0\), so controlling the error there automatically controls it everywhere in \(H_0\). Here we demonstrate this empirically: we compute the probability of a Type I error for several specific values of \(p\) within \(H_0\), for one decision rule, and observe that the maximum occurs at the boundary. A formal account, including worked examples and the underlying mathematics, is given in Jamshidian and Jamshidian (2026).

For the one-sided tests, the probability of a Type I error is largest at the boundary value of \(H_0\).

8.3.2 Obtaining Critical Regions and p-values Using Rguroo’s Probability Calculator **

In this subsection, we revisit examples from earlier in the chapter, but instead of using Rguroo’s Proportion Inference function, we compute critical values and p-values directly from the sampling distribution under \(H_0\) using Rguroo’s Probability Calculator function. This approach makes visible the area-under-the-curve interpretation of both quantities.

8.3.2.1 Using Inverse Probability to Find the Critical Region

When using a probability calculator, we could find a critical region by trying different candidate decision rules and checking their Type I error probabilities one at a time, as illustrated in Figure 8.21. For example, we might check whether the rule “\(X \ge 17\)” has a Type I error probability close to 0.05, then try “\(X \ge 18\),” and so on. While this trial-and-error approach works, there is a more direct method.

Many calculators provide an inverse probability function. A probability function starts with a value of \(X\) and returns the corresponding probability. An inverse probability function works in the opposite direction: we provide a probability, such as \(\alpha=0.05\), and it returns the value of \(X\) that creates that probability. This allows us to find the rejection-region boundary directly rather than testing several possible decision rules one at a time.

To find the critical region, we use the binomial distribution with the boundary value of \(p\) from \(H_0\) as the probability of success and \(n\) as the number of trials. Instead of providing a value of \(X\) and asking for a probability, we provide the significance level \(\alpha\) as the probability. The inverse probability function then returns the corresponding boundary value of \(X\), which we use to determine the critical region. Below, we show how this is done in Rguroo using its built-in probability calculator.

🔎 Solution

Since the alternative hypothesis is \(H_a: p > 0.70\), large values of \(X\) provide evidence in favor of \(H_a\). Therefore, we need a critical region in the upper tail of the binomial distribution. We use Rguroo’s Probability Calculator and the significance level \(\alpha\) to find the boundary value of \(X\) that defines this region.

8.4 Large Sample Hypothesis Test for One Population Proportion

In the previous section, we carried out hypothesis tests for a population proportion using the binomial distribution. That approach is conceptually clean and statistically exact: because \(X \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p_0)\) under \(H_0\), every probability we compute is precise. Generally, the binomial method is the ideal choice.

However, there are good reasons to also learn an alternative approach based on large sample theory; that is a method that relies on the central limit theorem and applies to large samples. First, and most importantly, the method generalizes naturally to other inference problems, comparing two population proportions, or drawing inferences about population means, where an exact distribution like the binomial is no longer available. Second, the large-sample method has historically been the most commonly reported, and you will frequently encounter it in published research. Finally, because \(X\) is discrete, the binomial method cannot always achieve the target significance level \(\alpha\) exactly; the actual Type I error probability may be somewhat less. The large sample method does not have this discreteness issue, since it uses the continuous normal distribution. For example, recall that in Example 8.10, the GSS2024 marijuana legalization example, the binomial method produced an exact significance level of 4.799% rather than the target 5%.

The large sample approach rests on the Central Limit Theorem (CLT), which you have already seen in Chapter ???. Recall that the CLT states that for large samples, the sampling distribution of the sample mean is approximately normal, regardless of the shape of the population distribution. We now take a moment to see exactly why it applies to sample proportions.

8.4.1 A Proportion Is a Mean

Suppose we record whether each individual in our sample has the characteristic of interest, assigning a value of 1 to each success and a value of 0 to each failure. For example, in the YouTube study, a student who uses YouTube daily receives a 1, and one who does not receives a 0. If our sample of \(n = 20\) students produced \(x_{\text{obs}} = 17\) daily users, the data would consist of 17 ones and 3 zeros.

Now notice something: the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) is simply the average of these zeros and ones:

\[ \hat{p}_{\text{obs}} = \frac{x_{\text{obs}}}{n} = \frac{\text{sum of zeros and ones}}{n} = \bar{x}. \]

This means \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\) is a sample mean; specifically, the sample mean of a collection of 0/1 values. Recall from the CLT that when the sample size is large, the sampling distribution of a sample mean is approximately normal, regardless of the shape of the original population distribution. Since \(\hat{p}\) is a sample mean, the CLT applies to it as well.

A Sample Proportion Is a Mean

The sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) is the mean of a collection of zeros and ones. Because the Central Limit Theorem applies to sample means when \(n\) is large, it applies to \(\hat{p}\) as well.

8.4.2 Applying the Central Limit Theorem to \(\hat p\)

Since \(\hat{p}\) is a sample mean of 0s and 1s, its sampling distribution follows the same general pattern as other sample means. For a population with proportion of successes \(p\), the sampling distribution of \(\hat{p}\) has:

  • Mean: \(p\)
  • Standard deviation: \(\sigma_{\hat{p}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{p(1-p)}{n}}\)

This standard deviation is often called the standard error of \(\hat{p}\). It measures how much we expect \(\hat{p}\) to vary from sample to sample. By the CLT, when \(n\) is sufficiently large, \(\hat{p}\) follows approximately a normal distribution:

\[ \hat{p} \sim N\!\left(p,\; \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}\right). \]

Since \(p\) is unknown, we need to specify a value for it to use this result for inference. Recall that in hypothesis testing we assume the null hypothesis is true, and that the maximum Type I error occurs at the boundary value \(p_0\) of \(H_0\). We therefore evaluate the sampling distribution of \(\hat{p}\) at \(p = p_0\), giving:

\[ \hat{p} \sim N\!\left(p_0,\; \sqrt{\frac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}\right). \]

We call this the null distribution of \(\hat{p}\), since it describes the behavior of \(\hat{p}\) at the boundary value \(p_0\) of \(H_0\), where the Type I error is at its maximum. Just as the binomial null distribution of \(X\) was the foundation for decisions in the previous section, this normal null distribution of \(\hat{p}\) is the foundation for decisions in the large sample method.

Most textbooks present the large sample test using a standardized statistic called the \(z\)-score, obtained by subtracting \(p_0\) from \(\hat{p}\) and dividing by the standard error:

\[ z = \frac{\hat{p} - p_0}{\sqrt{\dfrac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}}. \]

This unit-less quantity is then compared to a standard normal distribution. In this textbook, we instead work directly with \(\hat{p}\) and compare it to its null distribution. This requires fewer computational steps and, more importantly, keeps our decisions on the original scale — conclusions are about a sample proportion rather than an abstract standardized value.

8.4.3 What is Considered a Large Sample Size?

What constitutes a large sample size where the normal approximation to the binomial is good? A common rule of thumb is to check the expected numbers of successes and failures. In particular, the approximation is typically considered reliable when \(np_0 \geq 10\) and \(n(1 - p_0) \geq 10\). When both of these conditions are satisfied, the binomial distribution tends to be reasonably symmetric, and the normal model provides a good fit. A less strict guideline sometimes used is \(np_0 \geq 5\) and \(n(1 - p_0) \geq 5\), but this weaker condition can lead to noticeable inaccuracies, especially in tail probabilities. For this reason, the more conservative “10 and 10” rule is generally preferred in practice.

Large Sample Condition: The 10 and 10 Rule

The normal approximation for \(\hat{p}\) is considered reliable when both of the following hold:

\[np_0 \geq 10 \qquad \text{and} \qquad n(1-p_0) \geq 10.\]

These conditions ensure that the sample is large enough for the CLT to provide a good approximation. If either condition fails, the binomial method (see Section 8.2.1 and Section 8.2.2) is the appropriate choice.

To illustrate the 10–10 rule, consider the YouTube Daily Use example, where \(n=20\) and \(p_0=0.70\):

  • Expected successes: \(np_0 = 20\times 0.70 = 14\)
  • Expected failures: \(n(1-p_0) = 20\times 0.30 = 6\)

Since the expected number of failures is less than 10, the 10–10 rule is not satisfied. Therefore, the normal approximation may not be reliable for this example.

In contrast, for the Physical Inactivity example, where \(n=50\) and \(p_0=0.35\):

  • Expected successes: \(np_0 = 50\times 0.35 = 17.5\)
  • Expected failures: \(n(1-p_0) = 50\times 0.65 = 32.5\)

Since both values exceed 10, the 10–10 rule is satisfied. Therefore, the normal approximation is appropriate for the Physical Inactivity example.

8.4.4 Decision Based on a p-value

As in the binomial method, we define the p-value as the probability that \(\hat{p}\) belongs to the \(H_a\) support, assuming \(H_0\) is true:

\[ \text{p-value} = P\!\left(\hat{p} \text{ belongs to } H_a \text{ support} \mid H_0 \text{ is true}\right), \]

where the \(H_a\) support is the set of sample proportion value \(\hat{p}\) that are as favorable or more favorable to \(H_a\) than the observed sample proportion \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\).

For one-sided tests this is straightforward: if \(H_a: p > p_0\), larger values of \(\hat{p}\) are more favorable to \(H_a\), so the \(H_a\) support consists of \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\) and all values above it. Symmetrically, for \(H_a: p < p_0\), the \(H_a\) support consists of \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\) and all values below it.

For the two-sided test \(H_a: p \neq p_0\), the \(H_a\) support consists of values in both tails of the null distribution, symmetrically positioned about \(p_0\) due to the normal distribution being symmetric about \(p_0\) (see Figure 8.32). If \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} > p_0\), the \(H_a\) support is the set of values \(\hat{p} \geq \hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\) together with their mirror image relative to \(p_0\) in the left tail. If \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} < p_0\), the \(H_a\) support is the set of values \(\hat{p} \leq \hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\) together with their mirror image relative to \(p_0\) in the right tail. Figure 8.32 illustrates these two cases. In all cases, probabilities are computed using the normal null distribution.

8.4.5 Decision Based on a Critical Region

Recall from Section 7.6 that the critical region is the set of values of the statistic for which we reject \(H_0\) in favor of \(H_a\). The overall idea is the same as in the binomial method, but the statistic is different. Instead of expressing the critical region in terms of the count \(X\), we express it in terms of the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) and use the normal null distribution of \(\hat{p}\) to find the critical values.

For a one-sided test with \(H_a: p > p_0\), large values of \(\hat{p}\) favor the alternative hypothesis, so the critical region lies in the right tail of the null distribution. We find a critical value, denoted by \(\hat{p}^*\), that leaves probability \(\alpha\) in the right tail: \[ P(\hat{p} \geq \hat{p}^*)=\alpha. \] We then reject \(H_0\) if the observed sample proportion satisfies \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} \geq \hat{p}^*\).

Similarly, for a one-sided test with \(H_a: p < p_0\), small values of \(\hat{p}\) favor the alternative hypothesis, so the critical region lies in the left tail. We find \(\hat{p}^*\) such that \[ P(\hat{p} \leq \hat{p}^*)=\alpha, \] and reject \(H_0\) if \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} \leq \hat{p}^*\).

For a two-sided test with \(H_a: p \neq p_0\), both unusually small and unusually large values of \(\hat{p}\) favor the alternative hypothesis. The critical region therefore lies in both tails. We find lower and upper critical values, \(\hat{p}_L^*\) and \(\hat{p}_U^*\), that leave probability \(\alpha/2\) in each tail: \[ P\left(\hat{p} \leq \hat{p}_L^* \right)=\frac{\alpha}{2} \qquad\text{and}\qquad P\left(\hat{p} \geq \hat{p}_U^* \right)=\frac{\alpha}{2}. \] We reject \(H_0\) if \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} \leq \hat{p}_L^*\) or \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} \geq \hat{p}_U^*\).

8.5 Confidence Interval for a Population Proportion

We have seen that the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) is a natural estimate of the population proportion \(p\). However, because \(\hat{p}\) is based on a random sample, it varies from sample to sample and will rarely equal \(p\) exactly. This raises a natural question: given what we observed in our sample, what can we say about the range of plausible values for \(p\)?

To build intuition, think about what would happen if we repeated our study many times. Each time, we would draw a new random sample of size \(n\), compute a new observed sample proportion \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\), and use it to build an interval of plausible values for \(p\) around \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\). Because \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\) varies from sample to sample due to sampling variability, the intervals we construct would shift around as well. As a result, some intervals would contain the true value of \(p\) and some might not, depending on how far \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\) happens to fall from \(p\) in any given sample and the method that we use to build the interval. This is the unavoidable uncertainty that comes with basing our conclusions on a single sample. The key insight is that by choosing a specific, well-defined method for constructing the interval, rather than building it arbitrarily, we can control how often it succeeds in capturing the true value of \(p\) across many repetitions. The percentage of intervals that we expect to contain the true value of \(p\) when using such a method is called the confidence level. For example, a method that captures the true value of \(p\) in 95% of all repetitions produces what we call a 95% confidence interval.

Confidence Level

The confidence level is the percentage of times that the interval construction method is expected to capture the true population parameter, when applied repeatedly to many different random samples from the same population. In practice, we collect one sample and construct one interval, but the confidence level tells us how reliable our method is in the long run.

Notice that the confidence level describes the long-run behavior of the method, not any single interval. In practice, we collect one sample and compute one interval. That specific interval either contains the true value of \(p\) or it does not, we just do not know which. What we can say is that we used a reliable method: one that succeeds in capturing the true value of \(p\) at the stated confidence level across many repetitions.

Now that we have defined the confidence level, we can ask: how do we actually construct such an interval? We already have a tool that tells us whether a specific value of \(p_0\) is consistent with our data: the two-sided hypothesis test. If we test \(H_0: p = p_0\) versus \(H_a: p \neq p_0\) at significance level \(\alpha = 1 - \text{confidence level}\) and fail to reject \(H_0\), it means our data are consistent with \(p_0\). If we reject \(H_0\), it means \(p_0\) is not a plausible value of \(p\) given our data. This suggests a natural strategy: test a range of values of \(p_0\) and collect all those for which we fail to reject \(H_0\). The collection of all such values forms our confidence interval.

Confidence Interval by Test Inversion

Given an observed value \(x_{\text{obs}}\) from a sample of size \(n\), a \((1 - \alpha) \times 100\%\) confidence interval for \(p\) consists of all values \(p_0\) for which we would fail to reject \(H_0: p = p_0\) — using the observed data \(x_{\text{obs}}\) — in a two-sided test at significance level \(\alpha\). Notice that the significance level \(\alpha\) and the confidence level \((1-\alpha) \times 100\%\) are complementary: a 95% confidence interval corresponds to \(\alpha = 0.05\), a 90% confidence interval corresponds to \(\alpha = 0.10\), and so on.

The following example illustrates this idea concretely by working through a grid of values of \(p_0\) and identifying which ones are consistent with the observed data.

Example 8.27 (Daily YouTube Use Among Teens: Building a Confidence Interval from a Grid) Recall Example 8.1. A sample of \(n = 20\) students produced \(x_{\text{obs}} = 17\) daily YouTube users, giving a sample proportion of \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} = 17/20 = 0.85\).

The confidence interval that we obtained in the example above is the collection of all values of \(p_0\) that are consistent with our observed data at significance level \(\alpha = 0.05\). Notice that the interval is not symmetric around \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} = 0.85\): it extends further below \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\) (down to approximately 0.65) than above it (up to approximately 0.95). \[n\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} = 20 \times 0.85 = 17 \geq 10 \qquad \text{but} \qquad n(1-\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}) = 20 \times 0.15 = 3 < 10.\]

The condition fails because the expected number of failures is only 3, which means the binomial distribution is noticeably skewed in this example. As a result, the interval is asymmetric, and this asymmetry is real and should be reflected in our confidence interval. A method that forces a symmetric interval would miss this feature of the data and could give misleading results.

This example illustrates that the choice of method for constructing a confidence interval matters. In the subsections that follow, we study two approaches in detail. The first, presented in Section 8.5.1, inverts the exact binomial test to produce a confidence interval that correctly captures asymmetry when it is present. The second, presented in Section 8.5.2, uses the normal approximation and always produces a symmetric interval, which is accurate when the large sample condition is satisfied but can be misleading when it is not, as in this example.

A Quick Check for the Normal Approximation

Compare the midpoint of the exact binomial interval to the observed sample proportion \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\). Because the normal distribution is symmetric, a confidence interval built from a normal approximation is always symmetric about \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\) — its midpoint is exactly \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\). So if the midpoint of the exact binomial interval differs noticeably from \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\), the binomial distribution is skewed and the normal approximation may give misleading results.

8.5.2 Confidence Interval Based on the Large Sample Normal Approximation

In the previous section, we constructed confidence intervals using the exact binomial distribution. This approach is statistically precise and, as we saw, correctly captures any asymmetry in the interval when the large sample condition fails. With modern software like Rguroo, it is just as easy to compute as any other method.

However, long before computers were widely available, statisticians needed a way to construct confidence intervals that could be carried out with nothing more than a printed table of normal probabilities and a hand calculator. The key insight that made this possible is one we have already seen: when the large sample condition is satisfied, the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) is approximately normally distributed. This means we can replace the exact binomial calculation with a normal-based one, leading to a simple formula that can be computed by hand.

Why Learn the Normal Approximation Method?

Even though software makes the exact binomial interval easy to obtain, the normal approximation method remains important for two reasons:

  • It is the method most commonly reported in published research and popular media, so understanding it is essential for reading and interpreting results you will encounter in practice.
  • It connects naturally to confidence intervals for other parameters, such as population means, where an exact distribution is not available and the normal approximation is the standard tool.

Recall from Section 8.4 that when the large sample condition is satisfied, the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) is approximately normally distributed with mean \(p\) and standard deviation \(\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}\). In principle, the large sample condition requires that \(np \geq 10\) and \(n(1-p) \geq 10\). However, since \(p\) is unknown, we use \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\) in its place to check these conditions and to estimate the standard deviation of \(\hat{p}\). The resulting estimated standard deviation is called the standard error of \(\hat{p}\):

\[\text{SE}(\hat{p}) = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}(1 - \hat{p}_{\text{obs}})}{n}}.\]

The large sample condition is therefore checked as:

\[n\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} \geq 10 \qquad \text{and} \qquad n(1-\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}) \geq 10.\]

When this condition is satisfied, the \((1-\alpha) \times 100\%\) confidence interval for \(p\) takes the form

\[\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} \pm z^* \times \text{SE}(\hat{p}),\]

where \(z^*\) is the critical value from the standard normal distribution corresponding to the desired confidence level. The quantity \(z^* \times \text{SE}(\hat{p})\) is called the margin of error. It represents how far above and below \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\) we extend the interval, and it captures the uncertainty in our estimate of \(p\) due to sampling variability. A larger margin of error means more uncertainty — which happens when the sample size is small, when \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\) is close to 0.5, or when a higher confidence level is chosen.

Common critical values are:

Confidence Level \(\alpha\) \(z^*\)
90% 0.10 1.645
95% 0.05 1.960
99% 0.01 2.576
Large Sample Confidence Interval for a Population Proportion

When the large sample condition \(n\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} \geq 10\) and \(n(1-\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}) \geq 10\) is satisfied, an approximate \((1-\alpha) \times 100\%\) confidence interval for \(p\) is:

\[\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} \pm \underbrace{z^* \times \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}(1-\hat{p}_{\text{obs}})}{n}}}_{\text{margin of error}},\]

which gives the interval

\[\left(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}} - z^* \times \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}(1-\hat{p}_{\text{obs}})}{n}},\; \hat{p}_{\text{obs}} + z^* \times \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}(1-\hat{p}_{\text{obs}})}{n}}\right).\]

Notice that this interval is always symmetric around \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\), which is accurate when the large sample condition is satisfied but may be misleading when it is not.

8.6 Hypothesis Testing for Two Proportions

Many important research questions require comparing proportions between two groups. In medical research, clinical trials compare treatment outcomes between patients receiving a new therapy and those receiving a placebo or standard care. In the social sciences, researchers investigate differences between demographic groups. In educational research, teachers evaluate whether new instructional methods outperform traditional approaches. Understanding how to compare two population proportions allows us to evaluate whether observed differences in sample data reflect genuine population differences or are due to chance variation.

Consider a few recent examples: A Phase 3 trial of the mRNA-1273 vaccine (Moderna) with over 30,000 participants found that 0.4% of the vaccinated group developed symptomatic COVID-19 compared to 4.9% in the placebo group—a substantial difference. A 2022 study examining 1,166 articles in Political Psychology found that 35.1% of lead authors were women compared to 64.9% men, highlighting a gender gap in academic publishing. In each case, researchers collected data from two independent samples, observed different sample proportions, and found statistical evidence that the population proportions differed.

While these large-scale studies demonstrate striking differences, many research questions involve smaller samples and more subtle effects where the statistical evidence is less clear-cut. In this section, we develop the concepts and tools for comparing two population proportions by working with examples where the answer is not immediately obvious and careful statistical reasoning is required.

We extend the hypothesis testing framework from one proportion to two proportions. The same core concepts—null and alternative hypotheses, test statistics, p-values, Type I and Type II errors, and significance levels—continue to apply. However, we now focus on the difference between two population proportions rather than a single proportion.

8.6.1 The Null and Alternative Hypotheses

To develop these concepts and tools, we’ll work with four examples: an educational intervention comparing instructional approaches for teaching proportional reasoning, a health behavior study evaluating text message reminders for medication adherence, a workplace wellness program assessing nicotine patches for smoking cessation, and an analysis of gender differences in attitudes toward marijuana legalization from the General Social Survey.

When comparing two population proportions, we focus on the difference between them. We have two populations—let’s call one population 1 and the other population 2. It does not matter which population you designate as population 1 and which as population 2, as long as you clearly define your choice and interpret the difference \(p_1 - p_2\) accordingly. We denote the population proportion for population 1 by \(p_1\) and the population proportion for population 2 by \(p_2\). Our goal is to make inferences about the difference between these proportions: \(p_1 - p_2\). To do this, we begin by formulating hypotheses about this difference.

Just as in the one-population case, we can formulate both one-sided and two-sided tests. The three cases are:

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{Case 1: } &\begin{cases} H_0: p_1 \leq p_2 \\ H_a: p_1 > p_2 \end{cases} \qquad \text{Case 2: } \begin{cases} H_0: p_1 \geq p_2 \\ H_a: p_1 < p_2 \end{cases} \\ \\ \text{Case 3: } &\begin{cases} H_0: p_1 = p_2 \\ H_a: p_1 \neq p_2 \end{cases} \end{aligned} \]

We can equivalently write these hypotheses in terms of the difference \(p_1 - p_2\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{Case 1: } &\begin{cases} H_0: p_1 - p_2 \leq 0 \\ H_a: p_1 - p_2 > 0 \end{cases} \qquad \text{Case 2: } \begin{cases} H_0: p_1 - p_2 \geq 0 \\ H_a: p_1 - p_2 < 0 \end{cases} \\ \\ \text{Case 3: } &\begin{cases} H_0: p_1 - p_2 = 0 \\ H_a: p_1 - p_2 \neq 0 \end{cases} \end{aligned} \]

Cases 1 and 2 are one-sided tests, while Case 3 is a two-sided test. In practice, the choice of case depends on the research question—specifically, whether we are testing for an increase, a decrease, or any difference. Let’s see how to set up the hypotheses for each of our examples.

Example 8.36 (Schema-Based Instruction: Stating Hypotheses) Researchers want to know whether a new schema-based instructional approach improves seventh-grade students’ mastery of proportional reasoning compared to traditional instruction. They randomly assign 50 students to the new approach and 50 students to traditional instruction. Let \(p_1\) represent the proportion of all students who would achieve mastery under the new approach, and \(p_2\) represent the proportion who would achieve mastery under traditional instruction. State the appropriate null and alternative hypotheses to address the research question.

🔎 Solution

Since the researchers want to know whether the new approach improves mastery (that is, whether \(p_1 > p_2\)), this is a Case 1 situation. The hypotheses are:

\[ \begin{cases} H_0: p_1 \leq p_2 \quad \text{or equivalently} \quad H_0: p_1 - p_2 \leq 0\\ H_a: p_1 > p_2 \quad \text{or equivalently} \quad H_a: p_1 - p_2 > 0 \end{cases} \]

The null hypothesis states that the new approach is no better than (or is worse than) traditional instruction. The alternative hypothesis states that the new approach produces a higher mastery rate.

Example 8.37 (Text Message Reminders: Stating Hypotheses) Researchers are piloting a text message reminder system for diabetes patients. They want to know whether daily text reminders improve medication adherence compared to standard care, but they’re also open to the possibility that the reminders might perform worse (perhaps by being annoying or overwhelming). They randomly assign 40 patients to receive text reminders and 50 patients to receive standard care. Let \(p_1\) represent the proportion of all patients who would have good adherence with text reminders, and \(p_2\) represent the proportion who would have good adherence with standard care. State the appropriate null and alternative hypotheses.

🔎 Solution

Since the researchers have no strong directional prediction and want to detect a difference in either direction, this is a Case 3 situation. The hypotheses are:

\[ \begin{cases} H_0: p_1 = p_2 \quad \text{or equivalently} \quad H_0: p_1 - p_2 = 0\\ H_a: p_1 \neq p_2 \quad \text{or equivalently} \quad H_a: p_1 - p_2 \neq 0 \end{cases} \]

The null hypothesis states that there is no difference in adherence rates between the two approaches. The alternative hypothesis states that the adherence rates differ (in either direction).

Example 8.38 (Smoking Cessation: Stating Hypotheses) A workplace wellness program is comparing two approaches to smoking cessation. Employees are randomly assigned to either receive free nicotine patches (60 employees - Group 1) or participate in a self-guided quitting program (60 employees - Group 2). However, the researchers believe that the self-guided approach might actually be more effective because it emphasizes personal commitment and doesn’t rely on external aids. Let \(p_1\) represent the proportion of all employees who would quit smoking with nicotine patches, and \(p_2\) represent the proportion who would quit with the self-guided approach. State the appropriate null and alternative hypotheses to test whether the self-guided approach is more effective.

🔎 Solution

Since the researchers want to test whether the self-guided approach is more effective than nicotine patches (that is, whether \(p_2 > p_1\), or equivalently \(p_1 < p_2\)), this is a Case 2 situation. The hypotheses are:

\[ \begin{cases} H_0: p_1 \geq p_2 \quad \text{or equivalently} \quad H_0: p_1 - p_2 \geq 0\\ H_a: p_1 < p_2 \quad \text{or equivalently} \quad H_a: p_1 - p_2 < 0 \end{cases} \]

The null hypothesis states that nicotine patches are at least as effective as (or more effective than) the self-guided approach. The alternative hypothesis states that the self-guided approach produces a higher quit rate.

Example 8.39 (GSS 2024: Gender and Marijuana Legalization) The General Social Survey (GSS) is a long-running national survey that monitors societal change and studies the growing complexity of American society. In a representative sample from the GSS 2024 data, there are 204 females and 184 males who were asked whether they support the legalization of marijuana. Of the 204 females, 142 said they support legalization. Of the 184 males, 140 said they support legalization. Researchers want to know whether this sample provides evidence that the proportion of females who support legalization differs from the proportion of males who support legalization. Let \(p_1\) represent the proportion of females in the population who support legalization, and \(p_2\) represent the proportion of males who support legalization. State the appropriate null and alternative hypotheses.

🔎 Solution

Since the researchers want to know whether the proportions differ (in either direction) without specifying which gender should have higher support, this is a Case 3 situation. The hypotheses are:

\[ \begin{cases} H_0: p_1 = p_2 \quad \text{or equivalently} \quad H_0: p_1 - p_2 = 0\\ H_a: p_1 \neq p_2 \quad \text{or equivalently} \quad H_a: p_1 - p_2 \neq 0 \end{cases} \]

The null hypothesis states that females and males support marijuana legalization at the same rate. The alternative hypothesis states that the support rates differ between the two groups.

8.6.2 Data, Statistic, and Sampling Distribution

To make inferences about \(p_1 - p_2\), we collect a sample of size \(n_1\) from population 1 and a sample of size \(n_2\) from population 2. Note that these sample sizes don’t have to be equal—researchers often work with unequal group sizes due to practical constraints or deliberate design choices.

et \(X_1\) denote the number of successes (favorable outcomes) in our sample from population 1, and let \(X_2\) denote the number of successes in our sample from population 2. Although we could compare the counts through \(X_1-X_2\), differences in counts are difficult to analyze and can be misleading when the sample sizes differ. Instead, we compare sample proportions and rely on the Central Limit Theorem for large samples.

The sample proportions are \(\hat{p}_1 = X_1/n_1\) and \(\hat{p}_2 = X_2/n_2\). These are our point estimates of \(p_1\) and \(p_2\), respectively. To make inferences about the difference \(p_1 - p_2\), we naturally use the difference in sample proportions: \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2\).

The Sampling Distribution of \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2\)

When the sample sizes \(n_1\) and \(n_2\) are both sufficiently large, the Central Limit Theorem tells us that the sampling distribution of \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2\) is approximately normal. The rule of thumb for “sufficiently large” is that each group must have at least 10 successes and 10 failures: \(n_1\hat{p}_1 \geq 10\), \(n_1(1-\hat{p}_1) \geq 10\), \(n_2\hat{p}_2 \geq 10\), and \(n_2(1-\hat{p}_2) \geq 10\).

When these conditions are met, the sampling distribution of \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2\) is approximately normal with mean \(p_1 - p_2\) and standard error

\[ \hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 \sim N\left(p_1 - p_2, \; \text{S.E.}(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2)\right), \] where \[ \text{S.E.}(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2) = \sqrt{\frac{p_1(1-p_1)}{n_1} + \frac{p_2(1-p_2)}{n_2}}. \]

Notice that the variability depends on both sample sizes and both population proportions.

Simplification Under the Null Hypothesis

Recall that when testing hypotheses, we work at the boundary of the null hypothesis, since the Type I error is maximized there. For all three cases above, the boundary value is \(p_1 - p_2 = 0\), which means \(p_1 = p_2\).

When we assume \(p_1 = p_2\), let’s call this common value \(p\). Under this assumption, the standard error simplifies considerably:

\[ \text{S.E.}(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2) = \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n_1} + \frac{p(1-p)}{n_2}} = \sqrt{p(1-p)\left(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}\right)}. \]

Therefore, under the null hypothesis, the sampling distribution of \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2\) is:

\[ \hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 \sim N\left(0, \; \sqrt{p(1-p)\left(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}\right)}\right). \]

This is the distribution we’ll use for hypothesis testing.

Estimating the Common Proportion

There’s one practical issue: we don’t know \(p\), the common population proportion under \(H_0\). We estimate it from the data using the pooled sample proportion, which combines both samples into a single group:

\[\hat{p} = \frac{X_1 + X_2}{n_1 + n_2}.\]

This makes sense under the null hypothesis, which assumes both populations have the same proportion — so we treat them as if they came from a single population. Using \(\hat{p}\) in place of \(p\) gives the estimated standard error:

\[ \widehat{\text{S.E.}}(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2) = \sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})\left(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}\right)}. \]

This estimated standard error is used in our test statistic and in determining critical regions and p-values, just as we did in the one-population case.

Example 8.40 (Schema-Based Instruction: Computing the Key Quantities) In the schema-based instruction study, 50 students were assigned to the new approach and 50 to traditional instruction. After the intervention, 34 students in the new approach group achieved mastery, while 24 students in the traditional instruction group achieved mastery.

  1. Compute the sample proportions \(\hat{p}_1\) and \(\hat{p}_2\) and their difference.
  2. Verify the large sample conditions.
  3. Compute the pooled sample proportion \(\hat{p}\).
  4. Compute the estimated standard error of \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2\).
🔎 Solution

a. Sample proportions.

\[\hat{p}_1 = \frac{34}{50} = 0.68, \qquad \hat{p}_2 = \frac{24}{50} = 0.48, \qquad \hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 = 0.20.\]

The new approach shows a 20 percentage point higher mastery rate in the sample. We will test whether this provides sufficient evidence that \(p_1 > p_2\) or whether it could reasonably arise by chance when both approaches are equally effective.

b. Large sample conditions.

We check whether each group has at least 10 successes and 10 failures.

For Group 1 (new approach):

\[n_1 \hat{p}_1 = 50 \times 0.68 = 34, \qquad n_1(1 - \hat{p}_1) = 50 \times 0.32 = 16.\]

For Group 2 (traditional instruction):

\[n_2 \hat{p}_2 = 50 \times 0.48 = 24, \qquad n_2(1 - \hat{p}_2) = 50 \times 0.52 = 26.\]

All four counts are at least 10, so the large-sample conditions are satisfied and the Central Limit Theorem applies.

c. Pooled sample proportion.

\[\hat{p} = \frac{34 + 24}{50 + 50} = \frac{58}{100} = 0.58.\]

Under the null hypothesis, we estimate that 58% of students would achieve mastery if both approaches were equally effective.

d. Estimated standard error.

\[ \widehat{\text{S.E.}}(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2) = \sqrt{0.58 \times 0.42 \times \left(\frac{1}{50} + \frac{1}{50}\right)} = \sqrt{0.009744} \approx 0.0987. \]

This is the typical sample-to-sample variability in \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2\) if the null hypothesis is true.

8.6.3 Hypothesis Testing: Critical Regions and p-Values

As we explained in earlier chapters, there are two equivalent approaches to making decisions in hypothesis testing: the critical region approach and the p-value approach. In this section, we’ll develop both methods for comparing two proportions and illustrate how to implement them using Rguroo.

As noted earlier, for hypothesis testing, we work with the sampling distribution at the boundary of the null hypothesis. Recall that for all three cases, the boundary value is \(p_1 - p_2 = 0\) (that is, \(p_1 = p_2\)), since this is where the Type I error is maximized. Provided that the sample sizes are sufficiently large, at this boundary, the difference in sample proportions follows an approximately normal distribution:

\[\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 \sim N\left(0, \sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})\left(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}\right)}\right)\]

where \(\hat{p} = \frac{X_1 + X_2}{n_1 + n_2}\) is the pooled sample proportion. Both the critical region and p-value approaches use this distribution to determine whether the observed difference \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2\) provides sufficient evidence against \(H_0\).

A Note on Notation

In the one-sample sections, we wrote \(\hat{p}_{\text{obs}}\) to distinguish the observed value from the random variable \(\hat{p}\). For two proportions, that notation becomes cumbersome — we’d need \(\hat{p}_{1,\text{obs}}\) and \(\hat{p}_{2,\text{obs}}\). To keep things simpler, we use \(\hat{p}_1\) and \(\hat{p}_2\) for both meanings, and rely on context to make clear which one is intended: random variables when discussing the sampling distribution or computing probabilities, observed values when computing from data or making decisions.

The critical region approach asks: “Does the observed difference fall in the critical region?” The p-value approach asks: “How much support does the observed difference provide for \(H_a\)?” Both approaches lead to the same conclusion, and we’ll see how Rguroo can compute both simultaneously.

Let’s examine each case in detail.

8.6.3.1 Case 1: Right-Tailed Test (\(H_a: p_1 - p_2 > 0\))

In this case, we want to test whether \(p_1 > p_2\). The alternative hypothesis \(H_a: p_1 - p_2 > 0\) is supported when \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2\) is sufficiently positive. Values of \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2\) that are large and positive provide strong support for \(H_a\), while values near zero or negative provide little to no support for \(H_a\).

p-Value Approach:

The p-value measures how much support the data provide for \(H_a\). For a right-tailed test, the p-value is the probability of observing a difference that provides at least as much support for \(H_a\) as our observed difference, assuming we’re at the boundary of \(H_0\):

\[\text{p-value} = P(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 \geq \text{observed difference} \mid p_1 = p_2)\]

This is the area to the right of the observed difference in the \(N\left(0, \sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})\left(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}\right)}\right)\) distribution. A small p-value indicates strong support for \(H_a\), while a large p-value indicates weak support for \(H_a\).

Decision Rule: - Reject \(H_0\) if p-value \(< \alpha\) (observed difference strongly support \(H_a\)) - Fail to reject \(H_0\) if p-value \(\geq \alpha\) (data do not strongly support \(H_a\))

Critical Region Approach:

The critical region consists of values in the upper tail of the sampling distribution—these are the values that strongly support \(H_a: p_1 - p_2 > 0\). We find the critical value \(c\) such that at the boundary where \(p_1 = p_2\):

\[P(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 > c \mid p_1 = p_2) = \alpha\]

Using the sampling distribution at the boundary, we find \(c\) from \(N\left(0, \sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})\left(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}\right)}\right)\) such that the area to the right equals \(\alpha\).

Decision Rule: - Reject \(H_0\) if \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 > c\) (observed difference strongly supports \(H_a\)) - Fail to reject \(H_0\) if \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 \leq c\) (observed difference does not strongly support \(H_a\))

8.6.3.3 Case 3: Two-Tailed Test (\(H_a: p_1 - p_2 \neq 0\))

In Case 3, \(H_a\) is supported when \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2\) is far from zero in either direction. The p-value is therefore a two-tail probability, and the critical region occupies both tails with \(\alpha/2\) in each.

p-value: \(\quad \text{p-value} = P(|\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2| \geq |\text{observed difference}| \mid p_1 = p_2)\)

Critical values \(c_L, c_U\): \(\quad P(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 \leq c_L \mid p_1 = p_2) = P(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 \geq c_U \mid p_1 = p_2) = \alpha/2\)

Decision: Reject \(H_0\) if p-value \(< \alpha\), equivalently if \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 \leq c_L\) or \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 \geq c_U\).

Critical Region Approach:

The critical region consists of values in both tails of the sampling distribution—these are the values that strongly support \(H_a: p_1 - p_2 \neq 0\). We find two critical values, \(c_L\) and \(c_U\), such that at the boundary where \(p_1 = p_2\):

\[P(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 < c_L \text{ or } \hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 > c_U \mid p_1 = p_2) = \alpha\]

We split \(\alpha\) equally between the two tails (\(\alpha/2\) in each tail). Using the sampling distribution at the boundary:

  • \(c_L\) is the value with area \(\alpha/2\) to its left
  • \(c_U\) is the value with area \(\alpha/2\) to its right

Decision Rule: - Reject \(H_0\) if \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 < c_L\) or \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 > c_U\) (observed difference strongly supports \(H_a\)) - Fail to reject \(H_0\) if \(c_L \leq \hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 \leq c_U\) (observed difference does not strongly support \(H_a\))

8.7 Confidence Interval for the Difference Between Two Population Proportions

In Section 8.6, we learned how to test whether two population proportions differ. For example, in the schema-based instruction study (Example 8.41), we found strong evidence that the new instructional approach produced higher mastery rates than traditional instruction. The hypothesis test told us that a difference exists, but it didn’t tell us how large that difference might be. Does the new approach improve mastery by 5 percentage points? 20 percentage points? 35 percentage points? The hypothesis test alone cannot answer this question.

This is where confidence intervals become valuable. A confidence interval for \(p_1 - p_2\) provides a range of plausible values for the true difference between the two proportions, addressing the question “How large is the difference?” It tells us not just that a difference exists, but also gives us insight into the magnitude and direction of that difference. For instance, a confidence interval might tell us: “We are 95% confident that the new approach improves mastery rates by somewhere between 4 and 36 percentage points compared to traditional instruction.” This additional information helps researchers, educators, and policymakers make more informed decisions.

Just as in the one-population case (Section 8.5), a confidence interval for \(p_1 - p_2\) can be derived by inverting hypothesis tests: given the observed difference \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2\), the \((1-\alpha) \times 100\%\) interval consists of all values \(d\) for which we would fail to reject \(H_0: p_1 - p_2 = d\) in a two-sided test at significance level \(\alpha\). And as in the one-population case, the confidence level describes the long-run behavior of the construction method, not any single interval — a 95% method captures the true difference in 95% of repeated samples. In practice we use a direct formula based on the normal approximation rather than carrying out the test inversion by hand, but the logic is the same.

8.7.1 Large Sample Confidence Interval for \(p_1 - p_2\)

When the large sample conditions are satisfied, we can construct a confidence interval for \(p_1 - p_2\) using the normal approximation. Recall from Section 8.6.2 that when both samples are sufficiently large, the difference in sample proportions \(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2\) follows an approximately normal distribution.

However, there is an important difference between constructing a confidence interval and conducting a hypothesis test. In hypothesis testing, we assumed \(H_0: p_1 = p_2\) was true, which allowed us to use a pooled estimate \(\hat{p}\) for the common proportion. When constructing a confidence interval, we do not assume the proportions are equal—in fact, we’re trying to estimate how different they are! Therefore, we cannot use the pooled proportion.

Instead, for a confidence interval, we estimate the standard error using the individual sample proportions \(\hat{p}_1\) and \(\hat{p}_2\):

\[\text{SE}(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2) = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_1(1-\hat{p}_1)}{n_1} + \frac{\hat{p}_2(1-\hat{p}_2)}{n_2}}.\]

This is called the unpooled standard error, or simply the standard error, because we do not pool the two samples together.

Pooled vs. Unpooled Standard Error

The two-proportion confidence interval uses the unpooled standard error, based on \(\hat{p}_1\) and \(\hat{p}_2\) separately: \[\text{SE}(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2) = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_1(1-\hat{p}_1)}{n_1} + \frac{\hat{p}_2(1-\hat{p}_2)}{n_2}}.\]

The two-proportion hypothesis test, by contrast, uses the pooled standard error, based on the common proportion \(\hat{p}\) under \(H_0: p_1 = p_2\) (see Section 8.6.2): \[\widehat{\text{S.E.}}(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2) = \sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})\left(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}\right)}.\]

The reason is the assumption being made. The hypothesis test assumes \(p_1 = p_2\), so we pool. The confidence interval makes no such assumption — we’re trying to estimate the difference, not assume it away — so we estimate each proportion’s variance from its own sample.

The large sample conditions for a confidence interval are the same as those for hypothesis testing. We need at least 10 successes and at least 10 failures in each group:

\[n_1\hat{p}_1 \geq 10, \quad n_1(1-\hat{p}_1) \geq 10, \quad n_2\hat{p}_2 \geq 10, \quad n_2(1-\hat{p}_2) \geq 10.\]

When these conditions are satisfied, the \((1-\alpha) \times 100\%\) confidence interval for \(p_1 - p_2\) takes the form:

\[(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2) \pm z^* \times \text{SE}(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2),\]

where \(z^*\) is the critical value from the standard normal distribution corresponding to the desired confidence level, just as in the one-population case.

Large Sample Confidence Interval for \(p_1 - p_2\)

When the large sample conditions are satisfied, an approximate \((1-\alpha) \times 100\%\) confidence interval for \(p_1 - p_2\) is:

\[(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2) \pm \underbrace{z^* \times \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_1(1-\hat{p}_1)}{n_1} + \frac{\hat{p}_2(1-\hat{p}_2)}{n_2}}}_{\text{margin of error}}.\]

That is, the lower endpoint is \((\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2) - z^* \cdot \text{SE}\) and the upper endpoint is \((\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2) + z^* \cdot \text{SE}\), where \(\text{SE}\) is the unpooled standard error given above.

Common critical values remain the same:

Confidence Level \(\alpha\) \(z^*\)
90% 0.10 1.645
95% 0.05 1.960
99% 0.01 2.576

Relationship Between Confidence Intervals and Hypothesis Tests

There is an important connection between confidence intervals and two-sided hypothesis tests:

  • If a \((1-\alpha) \times 100\%\) confidence interval for \(p_1 - p_2\) does not contain 0, then we would reject \(H_0: p_1 - p_2 = 0\) at significance level \(\alpha\) in a two-sided test.

  • If a \((1-\alpha) \times 100\%\) confidence interval for \(p_1 - p_2\) contains 0, then we would fail to reject \(H_0: p_1 - p_2 = 0\) at significance level \(\alpha\) in a two-sided test.

This relationship provides a quick way to assess statistical significance: if zero is not a plausible value in your confidence interval, you have evidence of a real difference. This was evident in our examples: - Schema study: CI \((0.0105, 0.3895)\) excludes 0 → rejected \(H_0\) - Text message study: CI \((0.0548, 0.4252)\) excludes 0 → rejected \(H_0\) - Smoking study: CI \((-0.2771, 0.0771)\) contains 0 → failed to reject \(H_0\) - GSS study: CI \((-0.1530, 0.0234)\) contains 0 → failed to reject \(H_0\)

However, confidence intervals provide more information than hypothesis tests alone. They tell us not just whether a difference exists, but also the likely size and direction of that difference.
