In the previous chapter we learned how statistical inference works through count data and proportions. The same inferential ideas apply to means.

The framework is unchanged. We still begin with a research question about a population parameter, now the population mean \(\mu\) (read mu) rather than the population proportion \(p\). We still collect a random sample, compute a statistic from it, and study how that statistic varies from sample to sample through its sampling distribution. We still test claims by examining where our observed statistic falls within that distribution under \(H_0\), and we still make decisions using critical regions or p-values. Confidence intervals, significance levels, Type I and Type II errors, and confidence levels all retain the same meanings they had for proportions.

What changes is not the logic of inference, but the type of data we analyze and the distributions used to model sampling variability. Recall from the previous chapter that a sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) is itself a special type of sample mean: the mean of zeros and ones obtained from a binary variable. In this chapter, our variable is more general: a numerical variable whose values are measurements rather than indicators of success or failure. Just as the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) served as a natural estimate of the population proportion \(p\), the sample mean \(\bar{X}\) serves as a natural estimate of the population mean \(\mu\).

Because the underlying variable is no longer binary, the count \(X\) that anchored the previous chapter no longer applies, and neither does the binomial distribution that described its behavior. We therefore need new sampling distributions to describe how \(\bar{X}\) varies from sample to sample. We begin by assuming that the variable under study has a normal distribution, and later in the chapter we will use the Central Limit Theorem to extend these methods to a much wider class of numerical variables. The sections that follow develop these tools in detail and apply them to several running examples, mirroring the structure of the proportion chapter.

As in the proportion chapter, we will cover both one-population and two-population settings. In the one-population setting we draw inferences about a single population mean \(\mu\). In the two-population setting we compare two population means \(\mu_1\) and \(\mu_2\) through their difference \(\mu_1-\mu_2\). We begin with the one-population case.

10.1 Basics of Hypothesis Testing for a Population Mean

We begin our study of mean inference with hypothesis testing, following the same path we took in the previous chapter. We first establish the framework, then develop methods for making a decision based on either a critical region or a p-value. What will differ across the subsections that follow is the sampling distribution we use to make that decision, which depends on what we assume about the population.

Recall that \(\bar{X}\) represents the sample mean as a random variable because its value changes from sample to sample. Once we collect data, however, we obtain one specific observed value of the sample mean, which we denote by \(\bar{x}_{\text{obs}}\). This observed value is the statistic we use to make our decision in the hypothesis test.

10.1.1 Null and Alternative Hypotheses

To conduct a hypothesis, we begin by formally stating a claims we are investigating. This claim is formulated using the population mean which we denote by the Greek letter \(\mu\). Like in the proportion inference the null and alternative hypotheses always come in one of three forms, depending on the nature of the research question. Using \(\mu_0\) to denote a specific value of \(\mu\), the three cases are:

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{Case 1: } &\begin{cases} H_0: \mu \leq \mu_0 \\ H_a: \mu > \mu_0 \end{cases} \qquad \text{Case 2: } \begin{cases} H_0: \mu \geq \mu_0 \\ H_a: \mu < \mu_0 \end{cases} \\ \\ \text{Case 3: } &\begin{cases} H_0: \mu = \mu_0 \\ H_a: \mu \neq \mu_0 \end{cases} \end{aligned} \]

Here \(\mu_0\) denotes the bondary value stated in \(H_0\) and \(H_a\).

In Cases 1 and 2, the alternative hypothesis claims that \(\mu\) is either greater than or less than \(\mu_0\), pointing in a single direction. These are called one-sided (or one-tailed) hypotheses. In Case 3, the alternative hypothesis claims only that \(\mu\) is different from \(\mu_0\), without specifying a direction. This is called a two-sided (or two-tailed) hypothesis. As before, the form of the hypotheses determines how we set up our decision rules.

Let’s begin with a few examples.

Example 10.1 (SAT Math Scores at CSUF) According to the College Board, the mean score on the math portion of the SAT for the high school graduating class of 2025 was 508 points, with a standard deviation of 113 points. These figures are computed from the more than 2 million high school students who took the exam that year.

We wish to investigate whether the mean math SAT score among incoming freshmen in fall 2025 at California State University, Fullerton (CSUF) is higher than the national mean. Since CSUF does not require the SAT for admission, the exact record is not available, so we take a random sample of \(n = 40\) incoming CSUF freshmen and obtain a sample mean of \(\bar{x}_{\text{obs}} = 542\) points. Using this information, answer the following questions:

  1. What is the population of interest?
  2. Describe the parameter \(\mu\) in words.
  3. Write the null and alternative hypotheses.
🔎 Solution

  1. The population of interest is all incoming freshmen in fall 2025 at California State University, Fullerton (CSUF).

  3. The null and alternative hypotheses are as follows: \[ \begin{cases} H_0: \mu \leq 508 \\ H_a: \mu > 508 \end{cases} \]

10.1.2 The Null Distribution of the Sample Mean

As noted earlier, it makes sense to use the sample mean \(\bar{X}\) as a statistic for making inferences about the population mean \(\mu\). Like any statistic, the sample mean varies from sample to sample, and statistical inference is based on understanding this sampling variability through the sampling distribution of \(\bar{X}\).

To begin, we will assume that the variable under study has a normal distribution in the population. There are two reasons for starting with this assumption. First, the assumption is realistic in many practical settings — heights, weights, exam scores, and a wide range of biological and physical measurements are often approximately normally distributed. Second, under this assumption, the sampling distribution of \(\bar{X}\) takes a clean, exact form that allows us to focus on the main ideas of inference without the distraction of approximations or special cases.

In Section 10.4 we discuss how to assess the normality assumption using graphical methods such as Q-Q plots. Later, in Section 10.5, we will show that for sufficiently large samples the methods developed here remain approximately valid even when the population distribution is not normal.

As we saw in @sec-???, the sampling distribution of the sample mean \(\bar{X}\) depends on the unknown population mean \(\mu\). Since \(\mu\) is precisely the quantity we wish to make an inference about, we need a way to determine which value of \(\mu\) to use when working with this distribution.

As in proportion inference, in hypothesis testing we proceed by assuming that the null hypothesis is true, which determines the value or range of values for \(\mu\). For two-sided tests, \(H_0\) specifies a single value of \(\mu\), so we use that value directly. For one-sided tests, \(H_0\) consists of a range of values for \(\mu\). As discussed in the previous chapter and in Section 9.3.1, the boundary value of this range provides the largest Type I error probability among all parameter values allowed under \(H_0\). To ensure that the significance level is controlled conservatively, we therefore base our calculations on the boundary value \(\mu_0\) in all cases.

In summary, the sampling distribution of \(\bar{X}\) that we use in hypothesis testing is its distribution when \(\mu = \mu_0\). We refer to this distribution as the null distribution of \(\bar{X}\).

Under the normality assumption introduced earlier, there are two main cases for the sampling distribution of \(\bar{X}\) under \(H_0\). In the first case, the population standard deviation \(\sigma\) is known. In the second case, \(\sigma\) is unknown and must be estimated using the sample standard deviation \(s\). These two situations lead to different sampling distributions and inferential procedures, which we study in the subsections that follow.

10.2 Mean Inference When \(\sigma\) Is Known

In practice, the population standard deviation \(\sigma\) is rarely known exactly. There are, however, situations in which a value of \(\sigma\) has been established from extensive prior data and can reasonably be treated as known. The SAT example in Example 10.1 is one such case: the standard deviation of \(\sigma = 113\) points is computed from the scores of more than 2 million students who took the exam in 2025, a population large enough that we can reasonably treat this value as known when investigating the mean score among a particular subgroup such as incoming CSUF freshmen. Beyond examples of this kind, beginning with the known-\(\sigma\) case is helpful pedagogically because it allows us to focus on the main ideas of statistical inference while working with a simpler sampling distribution. Once these ideas are established, we will turn to the more realistic setting in which \(\sigma\) is unknown and must be estimated from the sample data.

10.2.1 The Null Distribution of \(\bar X\) When \(\sigma\) Is known

We first consider the setting in which the population standard deviation \(\sigma\) is known. Under the assumptions introduced earlier, the sample mean \(\bar{X}\) has a normal distribution when the null hypothesis is true. This distribution serves as the null distribution for hypothesis testing about the population mean \(\mu\).

The Null Distribution

 

Under the assumptions that

  • the variable under study has a normal distribution in the population,
  • the population standard deviation \(\sigma\) is known,
  • the sample size is \(n\), and
  • the boundary value specified by the null hypothesis is \(\mu = \mu_0\),

the null distribution of the sample mean \(\bar{X}\) is

\[ \bar{X} \sim N\left(\mu_0,\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right). \]

The result above tells us that, when the null hypothesis is true, the sample mean \(\bar{X}\) is centered at \(\mu_0\) and varies around it with standard deviation \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\). For instance, in the SAT example, the null hypothesis is \(H_0: \mu \leq 508\), then \(\bar{X}\) has a normal distribution centered at 508 with standard deviation \(113/\sqrt{40} \approx 17.87\) points. Roughly 95% of all possible sample means from random samples of size 40 would fall within about 35 points of 508 under the null hypothesis. Sample means much larger than 508 would be unusual under \(H_0\), and would provide evidence against it.

Some texts work with a standardized version of the sample mean, namely

\[ Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \sim N(0,1), \]

and for this reason the procedure is commonly called the \(z\)-test. Rguroo reports critical regions and p-values using both the standardized statistic \(Z\) and the sample mean \(\bar{X}\) on its original scale.

In this text, we will primarily use the null distribution of \(\bar{X}\) directly rather than its standardized version. Working on the original scale of the data allows us to interpret critical regions and p-values more naturally in the context of the problem and avoids the additional step of converting observations to standardized scores. In the SAT example, for instance, it is easier to reason about a sample mean of 542 points relative to a hypothesized population mean of 508 points than to translate everything into the unitless \(z\) scale.

10.2.2 Decision Based on the Critical Region Approach

To implement the critical region approach for testing hypotheses about \(\mu\), we need a rule that ensures the probability of a Type I error, when \(\mu\) equals the boundary value \(\mu_0\), is equal to a given significance level \(\alpha\). That is, the critical region must satisfy \[ P(\text{Rejecting } H_0 \mid \mu = \mu_0) = \alpha. \] The form of the critical region depends on the alternative hypothesis: large values of \(\bar{X}\) are evidence against \(H_0\) when \(H_a\) points to large values of \(\mu\), small values of \(\bar{X}\) are evidence against \(H_0\) when \(H_a\) points to small values of \(\mu\), and values of \(\bar{X}\) far from \(\mu_0\) in either direction are evidence against \(H_0\) when \(H_a\) allows departure in either direction. The three callouts that follow summarize the critical region in each of these cases.

Rejection Region for \(H_a: \mu > \mu_0\)


When the alternative hypothesis is \(H_a: \mu > \mu_0\), large values of \(\bar{X}\) provide evidence against \(H_0\). The rejection region therefore lies in the upper tail of the distribution of \(\bar{X}\) under \(H_0\) and has the form \[ \text{Reject } H_0 \text{ if } \bar{X} \geq c, \] where the critical value \(c\) satisfies \[ P\left(\bar{X} \geq c \mid \mu = \mu_0\right) = \alpha. \] Equivalently, \(c\) is the \((1-\alpha)\) quantile of the \(N\left(\mu_0,\,\sigma/\sqrt{n}\right)\) distribution.

Rejection Region for \(H_a: \mu < \mu_0\)


When the alternative hypothesis is \(H_a: \mu < \mu_0\), small values of \(\bar{X}\) provide evidence against \(H_0\). The rejection region therefore lies in the lower tail of the distribution of \(\bar{X}\) under \(H_0\) and has the form \[ \text{Reject } H_0 \text{ if } \bar{X} \leq c, \] where the critical value \(c\) satisfies \[ P\left(\bar{X} \leq c \mid \mu = \mu_0\right) = \alpha. \] Equivalently, \(c\) is the \(\alpha\) quantile of the \(N\left(\mu_0,\,\sigma/\sqrt{n}\right)\) distribution.

Rejection Region for \(H_a: \mu \neq \mu_0\)


When the alternative hypothesis is \(H_a: \mu \neq \mu_0\), values of \(\bar{X}\) that are either far below or far above \(\mu_0\) provide evidence against \(H_0\). The rejection region therefore lies in both tails of the distribution of \(\bar{X}\) under \(H_0\) and has the form \[ \text{Reject } H_0 \text{ if } \bar{X} \leq c_1 \text{ or } \bar{X} \geq c_2, \] where the critical values \(c_1 < c_2\) split the significance level evenly between the two tails: \[ P\left(\bar{X} \leq c_1 \mid \mu = \mu_0\right) = \frac{\alpha}{2}, \qquad P\left(\bar{X} \geq c_2 \mid \mu = \mu_0\right) = \frac{\alpha}{2}. \] Equivalently, \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are the \(\alpha/2\) and \((1-\alpha/2)\) quantiles of the \(N\left(\mu_0,\,\sigma/\sqrt{n}\right)\) distribution.

Finally, in the critical region approach, we reject \(H_0\) in favor of \(H_a\) if the observed sample mean \(\bar{x}_{\text{obs}}\) falls in the rejection region. Otherwise, we fail to reject \(H_0\).

10.2.3 Decision Based on the p-value Approach

A second approach to making a decision in a hypothesis test is based on the p-value. Conceptually, the p-value measures how strongly the evidence in our observed sample favors the alternative hypothesis, assuming that the null hypothesis is true. As in the proportion setting, we develop the idea of p-value through the \(H_a\) support: the set of values of the sample mean \(\bar{X}\) that are as favorable, or more favorable, to the alternative hypothesis than the value we actually observed.

To make this concrete, recall that our observed sample mean is denoted \(\bar{x}_{\text{obs}}\), and that we evaluate evidence using the null distribution \(\bar{X} \sim N\left(\mu_0, \sigma/\sqrt{n}\right)\). The \(H_a\) support depends on the form of the alternative hypothesis. The three callouts that follow summarize the \(H_a\) support and the corresponding p-value for each type of alternative hypothesis.

p-value for \(H_a: \mu > \mu_0\)


When the alternative hypothesis is \(H_a: \mu > \mu_0\), larger values of \(\bar{X}\) are more favorable to \(H_a\). The \(H_a\) support therefore consists of all values of \(\bar{X}\) at least as large as \(\bar{x}_{\text{obs}}\), and the p-value is \[ p\text{-value} = P\left(\bar{X} \geq \bar{x}_{\text{obs}} \mid \mu = \mu_0\right). \]

p-value for \(H_a: \mu < \mu_0\)


When the alternative hypothesis is \(H_a: \mu < \mu_0\), smaller values of \(\bar{X}\) are more favorable to \(H_a\). The \(H_a\) support therefore consists of all values of \(\bar{X}\) no greater than \(\bar{x}_{\text{obs}}\), and the p-value is \[ p\text{-value} = P\left(\bar{X} \leq \bar{x}_{\text{obs}} \mid \mu = \mu_0\right). \]

p-value for \(H_a: \mu \neq \mu_0\)


When the alternative hypothesis is \(H_a: \mu \neq \mu_0\), values of \(\bar{X}\) far from \(\mu_0\) in either direction provide evidence in favor of \(H_a\). Specifically, the \(H_a\) support consists of all values of \(\bar{X}\) whose density under the null distribution is less than or equal to the density at \(\bar{x}_{\text{obs}}\). Because the null distribution is symmetric about \(\mu_0\), this support consists of values at least as far from \(\mu_0\) as \(\bar{x}_{\text{obs}}\), and the p-value can be written as \[ p\text{-value} = 2 \times \begin{cases} P\left(\bar{X} \leq \bar{x}_{\text{obs}} \mid \mu = \mu_0\right) & \text{if } \bar{x}_{\text{obs}} < \mu_0, \\[4pt] P\left(\bar{X} \geq \bar{x}_{\text{obs}} \mid \mu = \mu_0\right) & \text{if } \bar{x}_{\text{obs}} \geq \mu_0. \end{cases} \]

In each case, the p-value is the probability, computed under the null distribution, of observing a sample mean at least as favorable to \(H_a\) as the one actually observed.

Once the p-value has been computed, the decision rule is the same regardless of the form of the alternative hypothesis: reject \(H_0\) if the p-value is less than the significance level \(\alpha\), and fail to reject \(H_0\) otherwise.

Intuitively, a small p-value means that the observed sample mean would be unusual under the null hypothesis, providing evidence against the null hypothesis. A p-value larger than \(\alpha\) means that the observed sample mean is reasonably consistent with the null hypothesis, and we have insufficient evidence to reject it.

It is worth emphasizing that the critical region approach and the p-value approach lead to the same decision: rejecting \(H_0\) using the critical region is equivalent to obtaining a p-value less than \(\alpha\). The two approaches present the same evidence in different forms. The critical region approach answers the question “for which values of \(\bar{X}\) would we reject \(H_0\)?”, while the p-value approach answers the question “given our observed value of \(\bar{X}\), how strong is the evidence against \(H_0\)?”.

10.3 Mean Inference When \(\sigma\) Is Unknown

As noted in Section 10.2, only in rare cases do we know the population standard deviation \(\sigma\). However, the good news is that the sample standard deviation \(s\) provides a good estimate of the population standard deviation, and statisticians have developed theory for using \(s\) in place of \(\sigma\) when the population distribution is normal. So, again, in this section we will assume that the variable under study has a normal distribution in the population that allows us to use this theory.

The theory developed for the case in which \(\sigma\) is unknown uses the so-called Student \(t\) distribution (or simply \(t\) distribution) which we will introduce next. We will compare the Student \(t\) distribution to the normal distribution that we have used for the known \(\sigma\) case and explain why the \(t\) distribution is needed when \(\sigma\) is unknown.

What has a \(t\) distribution?


It is important to be clear about what is assumed to be normal and what follows a \(t\) distribution. The assumption is that the variable under study has a normal distribution in the population. Under this assumption, the statistic \(\bar{X}\) follows a \(t\) distribution. In other words, the normality assumption is about the individual measurements, while the \(t\) distribution describes the behavior of the sample mean computed from those measurements.

10.3.1 The Student \(t\) Distribution

The name Student was actually the pen name of William Sealy Gosset, a statistician working for the Guinness Brewery in Ireland in the early 1900s. Because the brewery discouraged employees from publishing research under their real names, Gosset published his work anonymously under the name Student. His work led to a distribution that allows us to make reliable inferences about population means even when the population standard deviation is unknown.

The standard Student \(t\) distribution is similar in shape to the standard normal distribution: both are symmetric, bell-shaped, and centered at 0. However, unlike the standard normal distribution, the \(t\) distribution has a parameter called the degrees of freedom, denoted by \(\text{df}\). Different values of \(\text{df}\) produce slightly different shapes for the distribution.

Figure 10.12 compares several \(t\) distributions with different degrees of freedom to the standard normal distribution. When the degrees of freedom are small, the \(t\) distribution is more spread out and has heavier tails. As the degrees of freedom increase, the \(t\) distribution becomes closer and closer to the standard normal distribution.

The heavier tails of the \(t\) distribution are shown more clearly in Figure 10.13, which compares the standard normal distribution to a \(t\) distribution with 4 degrees of freedom. Notice that the \(t\) distribution places more probability in the tails and less probability near the center. This difference is important because, when \(\sigma\) is unknown, we replace it with the sample standard deviation \(s\). Since \(s\) varies from sample to sample, additional uncertainty is introduced into the inference procedure. The heavier tails of the \(t\) distribution account for this extra uncertainty.

The amount of additional uncertainty depends on the sample size. Small samples provide less information about the population standard deviation, so the uncertainty is larger and the \(t\) distribution has noticeably heavier tails. As the sample size increases, the sample standard deviation \(s\) becomes a more stable estimate of \(\sigma\), the additional uncertainty decreases, and the \(t\) distribution moves closer to the standard normal distribution. As we will see, the number of degrees of freedom in the context of mean inference is \(df = n-1\), related to the sample size.

Just as the standard normal distribution can be shifted and rescaled to obtain a general normal distribution with any center and standard deviation, the standard \(t\) distribution also has a more general form. We write this as

\[ X \sim t_{\text{df}}(\text{location}, \text{scale}), \]

where \(\text{df}\) denotes the degrees of freedom. The location parameter determines the center of the distribution, while the scale parameter controls its spread. In mean inference, the location will typically be the hypothesized population mean \(\mu_0\), and the scale will involve the estimated standard error based on the sample standard deviation \(s\), namely \(s/\sqrt{n}\).

10.3.2 The Null Distribution of \(\bar X\) When \(\sigma\) Is Unknown

We now turn to the more realistic setting in which the population standard deviation \(\sigma\) is unknown and must be estimated from the sample data. In this case, the null distribution of \(\bar{X}\) is no longer normal and instead follows a Student \(t\) distribution.

The Null Distribution

 

Under the assumptions that

  • the variable under study has a normal distribution in the population,
  • the population standard deviation \(\sigma\) is unknown and is estimated by the sample standard deviation \(s\),
  • the sample size is \(n\), and
  • the boundary value specified by the null hypothesis is \(\mu = \mu_0\),

the null distribution of the sample mean \(\bar{X}\) is

\[ \bar{X} \sim t_{n-1}\left(\mu_0,\ \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\right), \]

a \(t\) distribution with \(n - 1\) degrees of freedom, centered at \(\mu_0\) and with estimated standard error

\[ \hat\sigma_{\bar X} = \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}. \]

The result above parallels the known-\(\sigma\) case, with two changes. First, the normal distribution is replaced by a \(t\) distribution. Second, because \(\sigma\) is no longer available, we replace it with the sample standard deviation \(s\), so the scale of the distribution is the estimated standard error \(\hat\sigma_{\bar X} = s/\sqrt{n}\) rather than the known standard error \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\). As in the known-\(\sigma\) case, the distribution is centered at \(\mu_0\) when the null hypothesis is true, and sample means far from \(\mu_0\) constitute evidence against \(H_0\).

For instance, in the sleep example, suppose the null hypothesis is \(H_0: \mu \geq 7\) and we have a sample of size \(n = 30\) with sample standard deviation \(s = 1.2\) hours. Then \(\bar{X}\) follows a \(t\) distribution with \(n - 1 = 29\) degrees of freedom, centered at 7 hours and with estimated standard error \(1.2/\sqrt{30} \approx 0.219\) hours. Sample means much smaller than 7 with this scale would be unusual under \(H_0\).

The number of degrees of freedom of the \(t\) distribution is directly tied to the sample size: in mean inference, \(\text{df} = n - 1\). This relationship reflects the role of the \(t\) distribution in accounting for the extra uncertainty introduced by estimating \(\sigma\). When the sample is small, \(s\) is an unreliable estimate of \(\sigma\), the degrees of freedom is small, and the \(t\) distribution has heavy tails to reflect this added uncertainty. As the sample size grows, \(s\) estimates \(\sigma\) more reliably, the degrees of freedom increase, and the tails of the \(t\) distribution become lighter. In this way, the \(t\) distribution automatically adjusts the amount of uncertainty in the inference to match the amount of information in the sample.

Some texts work with a standardized version of the sample mean, namely

\[ T = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}} \sim t_{n-1}, \]

and for this reason the procedure is commonly called the \(t\)-test. Rguroo reports critical regions and p-values using both the standardized statistic \(T\) and the sample mean \(\bar{X}\) on its original scale.

As in the known-\(\sigma\) case, in this text we will primarily use the null distribution of \(\bar{X}\) directly rather than its standardized version. Working on the original scale of the data allows us to interpret critical regions and p-values in the units of the problem. In the sleep example, for instance, it is more natural to reason about a sample mean of 6.4 hours relative to a hypothesized mean of 7 hours than to translate everything into the unitless \(t\) scale.

When the \(t\) and normal distributions agree


As we saw in Section 10.3.1, the \(t\) distribution becomes closer to the standard normal distribution as the degrees of freedom increase. By the time the degrees of freedom reach about 30 — that is, a sample size of roughly \(n = 30\) or more — the two distributions are nearly indistinguishable, and the critical values and p-values they produce are essentially the same. For this reason, with large samples it makes little practical difference whether one uses the \(t\) distribution or the normal distribution, and the resulting inference is usually nearly identical. We nonetheless recommend using the \(t\) distribution whenever \(\sigma\) is unknown, regardless of sample size: it is always at least as appropriate as the normal, and it requires no judgment about whether the sample is “large enough.”
